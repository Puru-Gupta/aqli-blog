Across Southeast Asia, millions are inhaling air that is quietly shaving **## years** off their lives. The air throughout much of the region has become something people now check before stepping outside. In Bangkok, masks are no longer just a pandemic memory. In Vientiane, schools brace for haze days. Across the Mekong basin, the dry season has once again arrived with a familiar companion: fine, invisible particles that slip deep into lungs and shorten lives.

Air pollution is no longer an episodic environmental issue. It has become one of the region's most persistent threats to life expectancy, public health, and economic growth.

## Key finding

While seasonal haze often dominates headlines, Southeast Asia's air pollution challenge extends far beyond a few months of smoky skies. The recurring haze is increasingly a symptom of deeper structural issues, including fossil fuel use, biomass burning, and rapid urbanization.

Recent pollution episodes highlight both acute and chronic exposure across the region. In Bangkok, PM2.5 concentrations recently exceeded **86 µg/m³** in several districts. Vientiane recorded pollution levels far above national and WHO guidelines, prompting health advisories and emergency measures. Hanoi has repeatedly ranked among the world's most polluted cities during major pollution episodes.

Provincial data reveal that long-term exposure carries significant consequences for life expectancy. Residents of **Rakhine, Myanmar**, could gain **3.32 years** of life expectancy if PM2.5 levels met WHO guidelines. Similar gains are possible in **Bokeo, Laos (2.81 years)** and **Vinh Phuc, Vietnam (3.51 years)**.

## What changed

* Bangkok recently experienced PM2.5 concentrations above **86 µg/m³**, prompting public concern and mitigation measures.
* Laos has imposed a nationwide ban on outdoor burning during the dry season and increased enforcement against violations.
* Thailand has expanded cloud-seeding operations, industrial inspections, and construction-site controls during peak pollution periods.
* Jakarta continues to experience substantial health impacts from particulate pollution, associated with an estimated life expectancy loss of **## years**.
* ASEAN has strengthened cooperation on transboundary haze through expanded monitoring systems and the establishment of the ASEAN Centre for Climate Change (ACCC).
* Laos and Myanmar still lack formal national ambient PM2.5 standards despite some of the region's highest pollution exposures.

## Method note

Life expectancy impacts are based on the Air Quality Life Index (AQLI), which quantifies the effect of long-term exposure to fine particulate pollution (PM2.5) on life expectancy. PM2.5 concentrations refer to airborne particles with diameters of 2.5 microns or smaller. Examples presented here are drawn from city- and provincial-level pollution data across Southeast Asia.

Data sources include national air-quality monitoring networks, satellite-derived PM2.5 estimates, and AQLI calculations based on peer-reviewed epidemiological research.
