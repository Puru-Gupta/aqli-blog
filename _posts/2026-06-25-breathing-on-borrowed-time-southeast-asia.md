Across Southeast Asia, millions are breathing air that is quietly shortening their lives. What was once viewed as a seasonal nuisance has become one of the region's most serious public health challenges, affecting everything from daily routines to long-term life expectancy.

From Bangkok's smog-filled streets to haze-covered communities across the Mekong basin, fine particulate pollution (PM2.5) continues to expose millions of people to unhealthy air. As pollution episodes become more frequent and prolonged, governments are facing growing pressure to act.

## Key finding

Air pollution across Southeast Asia is no longer just a seasonal problem linked to agricultural burning and forest fires. The recurring haze that blankets much of the region each dry season reflects deeper structural challenges, including dependence on fossil fuels, biomass burning, and rapid urbanization.

Recent pollution episodes have highlighted the scale of the problem. In Bangkok, PM2.5 concentrations recently exceeded **86 µg/m³** in several districts. In Vientiane, pollution levels climbed far above both national and WHO guidelines, prompting health advisories and emergency responses. Hanoi has repeatedly ranked among the world's most polluted cities during major pollution episodes.

The consequences extend beyond temporary discomfort. In **Rakhine, Myanmar**, average PM2.5 concentrations reach **38.9 µg/m³**, and meeting WHO guidelines could increase life expectancy by **3.32 years**. In **Bokeo, Laos**, where average PM2.5 concentrations are **33.64 µg/m³**, residents could gain **2.81 years** of life expectancy. In **Vinh Phuc, Vietnam**, PM2.5 levels average **40.78 µg/m³**, corresponding to a potential gain of **3.51 years** if pollution were reduced to meet WHO recommendations.

## What changed

* Laos has imposed a nationwide ban on outdoor burning during the dry season and strengthened enforcement against violations.
* Thailand has elevated PM2.5 mitigation to a national priority, expanding cloud-seeding operations, industrial inspections, and controls on construction-related emissions.
* Jakarta continues to face substantial health impacts from particulate pollution, with long-term exposure associated with significant reductions in life expectancy.
* ASEAN has expanded cooperation on transboundary haze through improved monitoring systems and regional climate initiatives.
* The establishment of the ASEAN Centre for Climate Change (ACCC) aims to strengthen coordination between climate and air quality policies across member states.
* Despite high pollution exposure, Laos and Myanmar still lack formal national ambient PM2.5 standards.

## Method note

Life expectancy estimates are based on the Air Quality Life Index (AQLI), which quantifies the impact of long-term exposure to fine particulate pollution (PM2.5) on life expectancy. PM2.5 concentrations refer to airborne particles measuring 2.5 microns or smaller in diameter.

Examples presented in this analysis draw on city- and provincial-level pollution data across Southeast Asia and illustrate the potential life expectancy gains that could be achieved if pollution levels were reduced to meet the World Health Organization's air quality guideline.
